Concept
The Euclidean metric (Kronecker delta δᵢⱼ) is the rule that turns coordinate differences into distance: ds² = δᵢⱼ dxⁱ dxʲ = dx² + dy².
Serves
Describe Euclidean space as a geometric structure equipped with a metric; use the Kronecker delta as the metric in Cartesian coordinates; compute inner products and norms.
Inquiry
Will the straight-line Δs equal, exceed, or fall short of the grid-path |Δx|+|Δy|? Does translating both points change Δs? When does equality hold?
Probe
Prediction (a) "Δs = |Δx|+|Δy|" targets the taxicab/Manhattan-distance confusion, rebutted only when the student selects it.

Zoomed-in view

What you just did with Δx and Δy is the finite-displacement version of the infinitesimal rule. The matrix δᵢⱼ is the Euclidean metric — it tells you how to convert coordinate separations into distances.

ds, dx, dy are infinitesimals (the rule at a single point — symbols, never numbers). Δs, Δx, Δy are finite, measurable displacements. Same metric, two scales.

Metric (infinitesimal)
Einstein summation over i, j ∈ {1, 2}. The metric squares-and-sums coordinate differences.
Kronecker delta = the Euclidean metric
δᵢⱼ = 1 when i = j, 0 otherwise. In Cartesian coordinates the metric is just the identity.
Finite displacement (what you compute)
Signed differences; squaring means the sign never affects Δs.
Norm / inner product
The same metric measures the length of any vector: ‖v‖² = δᵢⱼ vⁱ vʲ.
What stays invariant
Rotate (3,4) by 90° → (−4,3): components change, the distance 5 does not. Translating both points leaves Δs unchanged too.
