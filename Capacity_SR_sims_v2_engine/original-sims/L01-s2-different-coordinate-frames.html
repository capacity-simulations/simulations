Concept
The same geometric point has different coordinate labels in different coordinate systems — coordinates describe how we label space, not an intrinsic property of the point.
Serves
Use Cartesian (and polar) coordinates to describe points and trajectories; understand coordinates as labels rather than physical observables; geometry as a mathematical description of space.
Inquiry
Given an (x, y) point, what are its coordinates in the (x′, y′) system? (Set up so it can be read off the grid.)
Reflection
The coordinates changed every time you moved the axes, but the point didn't move — so what exactly are coordinates describing?

Explore (lecturer build-up)

▾
  • Place a point: click either grid (up to 5). It appears at the same spot on both panels.
  • Read it off: the left label is (x, y), the right is (x′, y′) — two labels for one location.
  • Sweep θ: drag the slider (or press ▶ Play to auto-rotate). The S′ grid turns; the marker stays put while its (x′, y′) changes. Pause at θ = 0° (frames agree) and θ = 90° (axes swap).
  • Find the invariant: compare r and r′ under the panels — they always match.
Symbols: x, y — coordinates in Frame S · x′, y′ — coordinates in Frame S′ · θ — counter-clockwise rotation from S to S′.

Reflection

▾

The coordinates changed every time you rotated the axes — but the marker never moved. So what are coordinates actually describing?

They are labels we assign to a location once we pick axes, not a property carried by the location. The distance r is the same in every frame — that is the geometry beneath the labels.
