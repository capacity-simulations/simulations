Lecture
4 — Scalar invariants
Simulation
Simulation 1 — lecture visual aid (lecture-demo mode)
Concept
Vector components change under a rotation of Cartesian axes, but scalar quantities — each vector's norm and the norm of their difference — remain invariant.
Serves
Identify rotations as coordinate transformations between Cartesian systems; use the Euclidean metric to define invariants; distinguish scalars from vectors under coordinate transformations; derive the spatial distance (interval) as a scalar invariant.
Inquiry
What changes when the axes rotate, and what stays the same? Is the distance between two vector tips a scalar?

Guided inquiry

▾
  1. θ = 0°: both panels match — confirm the components are identical.
  2. Sweep θ: the arrows don't move, yet Aₓ, A_y diverge from A_{x′}, A_{y′}. What actually changed?
  3. Reveal ② Norms: sweep again — do ‖A‖ and ‖B‖ change?
  4. Reveal ③ ‖A−B‖: the distance between the two tips — is it a scalar? (It is the Euclidean spatial interval.)
  5. Drag a tip: norms update but still agree across S and S′.

Scalars vs. vectors

▾

A vector is a geometric object; its components are just how one particular set of axes describes it.

Rotate the axes → the components change. They are frame-dependent.

Invariant scalars: ‖A‖, ‖B‖ and ‖A−B‖ are the same number in every frame. That sameness is the Euclidean metric d² = Δx² + Δy².
