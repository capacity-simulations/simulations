Simulation
Simulation 1 — Lorentz transformations on vectors (mode: guided-inquiry)
Concept
A Lorentz boost changes a four-vector's components between inertial frames but preserves its Minkowski magnitude — the Minkowski-geometry analogue of a rotation preserving Euclidean length.
Serves
Define four-vectors and their transformation properties; relate four-velocity to ordinary velocity; describe how four-velocity transforms under Lorentz transformations.
Inquiry
Under a boost, what changes and what stays the same? Does invariance hold for timelike, spacelike and null vectors?
Misconception
That because the components change under a boost, the vector's "size" must change too — probed structurally by the prediction.

Key idea — what's invariant

▾

Components V⁰, V¹ are frame-dependent (they change with β). The Minkowski magnitude V·V = −(V⁰)² + (V¹)² is frame-independent — the invariant. A Lorentz boost is the Minkowski-geometry analogue of a Euclidean rotation: components change, the "length" does not.

Lorentz boost matrix (S → S′)

β ≡ v/c (boost velocity), γβ ≡ 1/√(1−β²).

Component transformation

V⁰ = time component, V¹ = space component (contravariant, superscript indices).

Minkowski metric & invariant magnitude

η = diag(−1, +1). V·V is the same number in every frame — a Lorentz invariant.

Four-velocity & velocity addition

U·U = −1. Ordinary velocity recovered as u′ = V′¹/V′⁰.

S′ axes on the diagram

t′-axis slope 1/β, x′-axis slope β — both tilt symmetrically toward the 45° light cone. Calibration ticks placed where each axis crosses t²−x² = ±n².
