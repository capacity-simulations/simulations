Concept
A symmetry transformation is a rotation that maps a shape back onto itself; different shapes admit different discrete sets of such angles, while the circle admits a continuous set.
Serves
"Describe spatial rotations as symmetry transformations" and "Recognise rotations as a prototype for more general symmetry transformations."
Inquiry

Reading the scene

▾
Ghost outline = the shape at θ = 0°.
Reference dot = tracks one vertex as it turns.
Gold pulse = θ is a symmetry angle (shape maps onto itself).
Coloured shape = current orientation at angle θ.

Key idea: discrete vs continuous

▾
A regular n-gon returns to itself at exactly n angles — θk = k·(360°/n) — so its order of rotational symmetry is n (3, 4, 5, 6 for these polygons).

The circle has every angle as a symmetry: its order is ∞ (continuous). That contrast — a discrete set of angles for polygons, a continuum for the circle — is the whole point of the demo. What stays invariant under any rotation is the shape's size and the distance of every point from the centre.
Rotation by θ (counter-clockwise, about the centre)
Symmetry angles of a regular n-gon
Triangle n=3 → {0°,120°,240°} · Square n=4 → {0°,90°,180°,270°} · Pentagon n=5 → every 72° · Hexagon n=6 → every 60°.
Order of rotational symmetry
The order equals the number of distinct symmetry angles in one full turn.
Length is invariant under rotation
e.g. rotate (3, 4) by 90° → (−4, 3); the distance √(3²+4²) = 5 is unchanged.
