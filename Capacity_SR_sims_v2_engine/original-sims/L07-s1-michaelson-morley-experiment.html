About: Michelson–Morley Interferometer

Source

Lecture
7 — Speed of Light
Simulation
Michelson–Morley experiment (Simulation 1), mode: guided-inquiry
Concept
The experiment's null result — no fringe shift on rotation — directly contradicts the Galilean velocity-addition prediction for light propagating through a luminiferous aether.
Serves
Aether; Theoretical Predictions; Michelson–Morley Experiment. Understand theoretical expectations for light in a medium; explain how Galilean relativity predicts direction-dependent light speed; identify the tension between Galilean relativity and electromagnetism.
Inquiry
Step 1: With the aether wind along an arm, does the parallel arm's round-trip take the same time as the perpendicular arm's? Step 2: Does rotation shift the fringes under the aether prediction, and does the actual experiment agree?
Misconception
"Light must travel faster relative to an observer moving toward it — just like sound does."

Step 1 — Round-trip times

The aether wind blows along the horizontal arm. A pulse in Arm 1 (orange) goes faster one way (with the wind, c+v) and slower the other (against it, c−v). A pulse in Arm 2 (blue) must aim into the wind (white arrow on the diagram).

Does the parallel arm's round-trip take the same time as the perpendicular arm's? Optional — commit a guess, then read t₁ and t₂ (now glowing) on the diagram.

Step 2 — Rotation & the null result

Slowly rotate the apparatus with the θ slider (now glowing). In Aether Prediction mode, watch the fringe strip shift (Δ varies as cos 2θ).

Now switch to Actual Experiment mode. The fringes never shift — at any θ or v. Galilean velocity addition fails for light: its speed does not depend on motion through a medium. There is no aether wind.

Symbols

▾

L = arm length  ·  c = speed of light  ·  v = aether wind speed  ·  β ≡ v/c

θ = apparatus rotation  ·  t₁, t₂ = round-trip times of Arms 1, 2  ·  Δ = path difference (in wavelengths λ)

Times are shown as multiples of 2L/c, the zero-wind round trip. The centre tick on the fringe strip is the no-shift reference.

Actual experiment (observed)

β ≡ v/c. This sim uses the exact Galilean difference √(1−β²sin²θ) − √(1−β²cos²θ) over (1−β²) for t₁ and t₂; the cos 2θ form is the standard leading-order approximation. With arm 1.1 m and v = 30 km/s the classical prediction is a non-zero shift (t₁−t₂ ≈ 3.7×10⁻¹⁷ s) — the actual experiment observed exactly zero.
