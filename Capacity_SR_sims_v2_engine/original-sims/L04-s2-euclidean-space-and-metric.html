About: Euclidean Distance as a Scalar Invariant

Source

Lecture
4 — Scalar invariants
Simulation
Simulation 2 — the Euclidean space & metric demo from Lecture 2, extended with controls that translate the origin and rotate the axes (guided-inquiry mode).
Concept
The Euclidean distance between two points is a scalar invariant — unchanged by translations or rotations of the coordinate system, even though each point's coordinates change.
Serves
Identify translations & rotations as coordinate transformations; use the Euclidean metric to define invariants; distinguish scalars from vectors; derive the spatial distance (interval) as a scalar invariant; treat invariance as the guide for constructing physical observables.
Inquiry
Are Δx, Δy invariant under translation? Under rotation? What combination of them is invariant under both — and why?
Misconception
"If the coordinates change, the distance computed from them must change too." (Encoded as prediction choices a/b; rebutted only on selection.)
Kick-off
Students formulate their own definition of "physical observable" from the simulation evidence before the formal one is given (Claudia's prompt).

A scalar (d) is invariant under both. A vector's components survive translation but not rotation. Raw coordinates survive neither — they can't be physical observables.
