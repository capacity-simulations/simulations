Simulation
Simulation 1 — Ball on a train (guided-inquiry mode)
Concept
The same event — a ball tossed vertically on a moving train — is described by different trajectories in different inertial frames, yet both descriptions are equally valid.
Serves
Elements: Static Observers, Equivalence, Relativity, Relative Time. Outcomes: build intuition for the equivalence of inertial observers; appreciate that physical laws take the same form in all inertial frames; identify the motivation for (and limits of pre-) special relativity.
Inquiry
Sketch the ball's path as seen from the train, then from the platform. Are both describing the same physical event? Can both be correct at once?
Misconception
(1) "It goes straight up in both frames." (2) "Once airborne the ball is left behind by the train." Both encoded as selectable wrong predictions.

Predict (optional)

Exploration guide

▾

Inquiry

▾

Sketch (in your head, or on paper) the ball's path as seen from the train. Now sketch it as seen from the platform.

Are both sketches describing the same physical event? Can both be correct at the same time? (They can — this is the principle of equivalence between inertial observers.)

Going further: suppose we threw a light pulse instead of a ball. Would the same "just add the train's velocity" logic still work? Hold that thought — it is where special relativity begins.

Symbols

▾
  • v — train speed relative to the platform
  • u — ball's throw speed relative to the train
  • x, y — ball position in the active frame
  • vₓ, vᵧ — ball velocity components in the active frame
  • g — gravity = 9.8 m/s² (downward)
  • T = 2u/g — flight time  ·  u²/(2g) — peak height

All Galilean — there are no relativistic corrections here: no Lorentz factor, no length contraction, no time dilation. Velocities simply add.

Galilean position transform (train → platform)
Horizontal description changes; vertical position is the same.
Galilean velocity transform
For a vertical throw vx,train = 0, so vx,plat = v. vy is the same.
Galilean velocity addition (plain sum)
w′ = any velocity measured in the moving frame. E.g. w′ = 8 m/s on the train + v = 12 m/s ⇒ 20 m/s on the ground. (Not to be confused with u, the throw speed.)
Projectile motion (both frames)
Identical vertical physics in either frame.
Frame-invariant quantities
Flight time, peak height, vertical velocity and acceleration — same for everyone.
Frame-dependent quantities
Straight line (train) vs. parabola (platform) — two valid descriptions of one event.
