Concept
The spacetime interval Δs² = Δt² − Δx² (c = 1) defines a non-Euclidean "distance" that can be negative, zero, or positive — classifying event pairs as timelike, lightlike, or spacelike.
Serves
Describe Minkowski spacetime and its metric structure; identify invariant quantities under Lorentz transformations (the interval is the central invariant).
Inquiry
Place P₂ at spacelike / timelike / lightlike separations from P₁, read Δt and Δx off the grid, compute Δs², and watch the sign flip as the pair crosses the light cone.
Misconception
"A squared distance is always ≥ 0" — the Euclidean intuition. The Minkowski minus sign makes Δs² < 0 for spacelike pairs.

Predict — sign of the interval

▾

As you drag P₂ from far out in space (Δx > Δt) to inside the light cone (Δt > Δx), the interval Δs² = Δt² − Δx² changes.

In Euclidean geometry a squared distance is always ≥ 0. What does Δs² do across this drag?

Optional — the sim is fully usable whether or not you answer.

Investigate — read, compute, connect

▾

1. Drag P₂ far to the side so Δx > Δt. Read Δt and Δx off the grid, type them below the diagram, press Compute. Δs² < 0 → spacelike.

2. Drag P₂ inside the light cone so Δt > Δx. Compute again: Δs² > 0 → timelike.

3. Land P₂ exactly on a dashed 45° line (|Δt| = |Δx|). Δs² = 0 → lightlike.

4. Switch to the Local metric view (top-left) and rotate the direction through 45° — watch the coloured ds shrink to zero on the light cone.

Symbols & notation

▾
Δt, Δx — finite coordinate differences between P₁ and P₂ (light-seconds).
Δs² — finite squared interval = Δt² − Δx² (ls²). The invariant interval: same in every inertial frame.
dt, dx — infinitesimal displacements (Local view). Symbols, not numbers.
ds² — infinitesimal squared interval = dt² − dx².
ημν — Minkowski metric tensor, diag(+1, −1) in the (t, x) basis.
Convention: signature (+, −), c = 1 ⇒ Δs² > 0 timelike, = 0 lightlike, < 0 spacelike.

Invariance
Δs² is a Lorentz-invariant scalar — all inertial observers compute the same value even though they disagree on Δt and Δx separately. (The next simulation demonstrates this under a Lorentz boost.)
