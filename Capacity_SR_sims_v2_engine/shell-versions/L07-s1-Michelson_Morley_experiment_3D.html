About: Michelson–Morley Interferometer (3D)

About This Simulation

This interactive 3D simulation reproduces the Michelson–Morley experiment (1887) as it was actually built: the optics are bolted to a massive stone slab that floats on liquid mercury in an annular cast-iron trough on a brick pier, so the whole apparatus can be rotated smoothly.

Light from the source is split by a half-silvered mirror at 45° (the beam splitter, BS). One beam passes through a compensating plate to mirror M₁; the other reflects to mirror M₂. The compensator is an identical, unsilvered copy of the splitter, so both beams traverse the same thickness of glass — without it, white-light fringes could not be seen. The returning beams recombine at BS and interfere in the telescope detector.

In the 19th century, physicists believed light traveled through a medium called the “luminiferous aether.” If so, the Earth’s motion through this aether would create a “wind” that changes the effective speed of light differently in each arm, producing a measurable fringe shift when the slab is rotated.

Aether Prediction mode shows the expected fringe shift using Galilean velocity addition — this prediction is wrong.

Actual (Relativity) mode shows the real result: no fringe shift at any angle. FitzGerald and Lorentz first explained this with an ad-hoc length contraction of the arm along the motion; Einstein’s Special Relativity (1905) explained it fundamentally — the speed of light is the same in all directions for every inertial observer.

β (v/c) is fixed at Earth’s real orbital speed (β ≈ 9.93×10⁻⁵). The effect is minuscule: with ~11 m arms the predicted round-trip difference is only a few hundred attoseconds, so the readout shows it in scientific notation / attoseconds (and it is exactly 0 in Actual mode — the null result). Guided Inquiry

1 · Meet the apparatus

Light from the source hits the beam splitter and runs down two perpendicular arms to mirrors A and B, then returns. In Experiment view you read each arm's round-trip time; in Simplified view a single beam goes out to one mirror and back. The red arrows are the hypothetical aether wind.

Drag to orbit · scroll to zoom.

2 · Predict

As the apparatus turns, each arm's angle to the wind changes, so the along-wind and cross-wind light speeds trade places. In Aether mode, what should the two arms' round-trip times do as you rotate θ?

3 · See the predicted difference

In Aether mode, drag the Rotation θ slider (highlighted) — or press Auto Rotate. In the readout, the predicted A and B round-trip times differ, and the A − B difference (in attoseconds) traces cos 2θ: zero at 45°, peaking at ≈ ±362 as along and across the wind. The two times differ only in their 8th digit — which is exactly why the effect was so hard to detect.

Optional: change Arm length L — the round-trip times and their A−B difference scale with it.

4 · The null result

The sim has switched to Actual (Relativity) mode. Rotate again — or Auto Rotate — and the two round-trip times stay exactly equal: A − B = 0 at every angle. Both arms always take 2L/c, because light's speed doesn't depend on motion through any medium.

"Light must travel faster relative to an observer moving toward it — just like sound does."
