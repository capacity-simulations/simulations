About: Time Dilation — Light Clock (3D)

About This Simulation

A 3D rendering of the classic light-clock thought experiment: a single photon bounces between two mirrors mounted inside a moving train, and one physical scene is watched from two reference frames — switch between them with the toggle in the sidebar.

Train frame — the camera rides inside the glass compartment. The photon simply bounces straight up and down between the mirrors while the night landscape streams past the windows.

Platform frame — the camera stands still on the station platform. The train (visibly length-contracted by 1/γ) rushes past, and the very same photon must now chase the moving mirrors, tracing a longer diagonal zigzag path.

Rest-frame tick: TA = 2h / c · Platform-frame tick: TB = 2hγ / c = γ · TA · γ = 1 / √(1 − β²) , θ = arcsin β.

Because the speed of light c is the same in both frames, the longer diagonal path must take longer — so the moving clock ticks slowly. The digital readouts and the graph in the sidebar show tB = tA/γ falling behind in real time.

Adjust β = v/c and the mirror separation h with the sliders. The run resets automatically when the train leaves the station scene.

Guided Inquiry

1 · Predict

Observer A (on the train) and B (on the platform) each time the same light pulse's round trip. Who measures the longer time?

2 · Train frame — proper time

You're riding inside the train (A's frame). The pulse bounces straight up and down; the round trip is tA = 2h/c — the proper time, because emission and return happen at the same place.

3 · Platform frame — the diagonal

Now stand on the platform (B's frame). The train is length-contracted to 1/γ and rushes past; the same pulse traces a diagonal (height h plus a horizontal leg) at the same speed c. Longer path ⇒ tB > tA.

4 · Vary v, watch γ

Drag β = v/c (highlighted) and watch γ and the graph. At v = 0 the times are equal; as v → c, tB → ∞. The height h (transverse to motion) never changes — only the diagonal path and the time grow.

5 · A friend says…

"In B's frame A is moving, so A's clock runs slow — that means B's clock runs fast relative to A's."

Back in the train frame: according to A, who is moving, and whose clock runs slow?
