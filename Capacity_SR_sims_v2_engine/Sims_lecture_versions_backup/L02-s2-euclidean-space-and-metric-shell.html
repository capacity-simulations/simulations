Euclidean Metric & Distance
The metric at a point
δᵢⱼ (Kronecker delta) = 1 when i = j, 0 otherwise — this 2×2 identity matrix is the Euclidean metric.

Guided Inquiry

1 · How far apart are P₁ and P₂?

You're zoomed out on the plane. Drag P₁ or P₂ (they snap to integers) and read their coordinates. The straight-line distance isn't the sum of the legs — the metric combines the coordinate differences: Δs² = Δx² + Δy². Type Δx and Δy in Controls to compute Δs.

2 · Does where the segment sits matter?

Note the current Δx, Δy and Δs. Now rebuild the same Δx and Δy somewhere else on the grid. Will Δs come out the same?

3 · The same rule, infinitesimally

Switch to Zoomed in. The very same rule applies to infinitesimal legs: ds² = dx² + dy². This is the metric at a point — the matrix δᵢⱼ (the Kronecker delta / identity) is the Euclidean metric. Finite Δs and infinitesimal ds are the same rule at two scales.

4 · What a metric is — and a look ahead

A metric is just a rule that turns coordinate differences into a distance. Here it's the identity δᵢⱼ, so every term is +: ds² = dx² + dy².

Spacetime uses the same idea with one twist — the Minkowski metric flips the sign on the time term: ds² = −(c·dt)² + dx². Toggle Zoomed out ⇄ in to compare the finite and infinitesimal pictures, then hit Finish to explore freely.

Metric (infinitesimal)
Einstein summation over i, j ∈ {1, 2}. The metric squares-and-sums coordinate differences.
Kronecker delta = the Euclidean metric
δᵢⱼ = 1 when i = j, 0 otherwise. In Cartesian coordinates the metric is just the identity.
Finite displacement (what you compute)
Signed differences; squaring means the sign never affects Δs.
Norm / inner product
The same metric measures the length of any vector: ‖v‖² = δᵢⱼ vⁱ vʲ.
What stays invariant
Rotate (3,4) by 90° → (−4,3): components change, the distance 5 does not. Translating both points leaves Δs unchanged too.
