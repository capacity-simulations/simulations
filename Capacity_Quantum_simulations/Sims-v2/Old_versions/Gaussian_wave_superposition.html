About This Simulation

Two Gaussian wavepackets $\psi_1$ and $\psi_2$ centered at positions $x_1$ and $x_2$ are combined into a superposition. Each wavepacket is a normalized Gaussian:

$$\psi_n(x) = \left(2\pi\sigma^2\right)^{-1/4} \exp\!\left[-\frac{(x - x_n)^2}{4\sigma^2}\right]$$

Because these Gaussian wavepackets are non-orthogonal (they overlap in space), they cannot be assigned different energy eigenvalues without breaking unitarity and probability conservation. To maintain physical validity, both wavepackets are treated as degenerate states sharing the same energy $E$:

$$\Psi(x,t) = \left(c_1\,\psi_1(x) + c_2\,\psi_2(x)\right) e^{-iEt/\hbar}$$

Because both components share the same phase factor $e^{-iEt}$, the probability density is completely stationary (time-independent):

$$|\Psi(x,t)|^2 = \left|c_1\,\psi_1(x) + c_2\,\psi_2(x)\right|^2$$

The common phase factor cancels out in the modulus squared. Only the real part $\text{Re}(\Psi)$ oscillates — and all components oscillate in perfect unison at frequency $E/\hbar$.

The coefficients are properly normalized accounting for the non-orthogonal overlap:

$$|c_1|^2 + |c_2|^2 + 2\,c_1 c_2\,\langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle = 1$$

The spatial overlap of the two Gaussians is:

$$\langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle = \exp\!\left[-\frac{(x_1 - x_2)^2}{8\sigma^2}\right]$$
