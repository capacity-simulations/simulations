About This Simulation

This interactive visualization demonstrates how complex waveforms can be constructed by summing simple sine waves — the core idea behind Fourier series.

Choose a square wave, sawtooth wave, or Gaussian in a box and adjust the number of terms (N). The top panel shows each individual sine component with its amplitude and frequency. The bottom panel overlays the partial Fourier sum (colored line) against the ideal target (dashed line).

The square and sawtooth waves have discontinuities whose coefficients decay as 1/n, producing the Gibbs phenomenon (~9% overshoot even as N→∞). The Gaussian's coefficients decay super-exponentially, so convergence is dramatically faster — compare the two regimes by switching wave types.

The equation bar at the top displays the current partial sum formula up to N terms.
